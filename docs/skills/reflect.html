reflect: Guided Self-Reflection for Loop

skill View on GitHub

A Goose skill that guides you through writing meaningful self-reflections for Loop within The Hub. Instead of generating content for you, it prompts you with thoughtful questions—you do the thinking and writing.

Quick Start

| Command | Description | |---------|-------------| | /reflect | Start a guided reflection session |

What It Does

reflect transforms the often-rushed task of writing Loop reflections into a thoughtful, guided exercise:

  • Prompts, not generates — You write your own reflection; the skill just asks good questions
  • One question at a time — No overwhelming forms; just a conversation
  • Goes deeper — Questions designed for insight, not just recap
  • Compiles your words — Formats your responses for easy copy/paste to Loop in The Hub

About Loop in The Hub

Loop is your company's tool for continuous feedback and reflection, accessible through The Hub. The reflection prompt asks:

"Reflect on your experiences, growth, and contributions. This will be available to you and your manager."

This skill helps you write a meaningful response to that prompt.

The Reflection Framework

The skill guides you through five areas:

| Section | Focus | Example Prompt | |---------|-------|----------------| | Impact & Meaning | What mattered most | "What work felt most meaningful? Why?" | | Relationships & Influence | People and interactions | "Who made an impression on you?" | | Growth & Insight | Learning and surprises | "What challenged an assumption you had?" | | Intention & Direction | What is next | "What is pulling at your attention?" | | Closing | Overall state | "How are you feeling about where you are?" |

Why This Approach?

Most people treat Loop reflections as a recap—listing what they did. But reflection is about meaning—understanding why it mattered and what you are taking forward.

This skill shifts the questions from:

  • "What did you do?" becomes "What felt meaningful?"
  • "Who did you work with?" becomes "Who made an impression on you?"
  • "What did you learn?" becomes "What surprised you or challenged an assumption?"

Tips for Better Reflections

1. Be Specific

Instead of "worked on the project," name the project, the specific contribution, and the outcome.

2. Name People

Reflections that mention colleagues by name are more meaningful—both for you and for your manager reading them.

3. Sit With the Questions

These are not meant to be answered instantly. If a question makes you pause, that is good.

4. It is Okay to Say "Nothing"

Not every week has a breakthrough insight. Acknowledging a quiet week is valid.

5. Find Your Cadence

Some people reflect weekly, others bi-weekly. Find what works for you and stick to it.

Suggested Cadence

| Frequency | Best For | |-----------|----------| | Weekly | Fast-moving projects, new roles, growth-focused periods | | Bi-weekly | Steady-state work, established routines | | Ad-hoc | After major milestones, project completions, or significant events |

Background

This skill was designed around the idea that reflection is different from recap:

  • Recap = What happened (facts)
  • Reflection = What it meant (insight)

The questions are intentionally open-ended to encourage deeper thinking rather than checkbox completion.

---

Remember: The goal is not to write the perfect reflection—it is to take a few minutes to actually think about your week. The writing is just how you capture it.
